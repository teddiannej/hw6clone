The course deals with the question “what are the basic mathematical concepts and techniques needed in computer science?” It provides an introduction to proof principles and logics, functions and relations, induction principles, combinatorics and graph theory, as well as a rigorous grounding in writing and reading mathematical proof.

Reviews of this course were quite diverse, as students characterized it as “interesting,” “challenging,” “difficult,” “a waste of time,” and “curious.” The content of the course was described by most students to be “confusing,” “abstract,” “dense,” and “hard to follow.” While some students seemed to enjoy the course, others found it to be pointless given that they spent most of their time “trying to figure out how to do problems” rather than “learning the material.” Several students felt that the homework assignments were extremely challenging, stating that they were “bordering on impossible.” However, students reported that the professors and the TAs gave away the answers and the solutions during office hours, making it easy to do well in the course. Many students were, however, dissatisfied with the grading system.

Students’ opinions about Professor Gallier were mixed. Students generally appreciated his amazing sense of humor and his constant jokes and anecdotes, which they felt made the class enjoyable. However, many thought that he moved through the material too quickly, did not lecture in a clear way, and did not provide sufficient examples to tackle the homework problems. Some reported that since he used his own notation and tree approach for proofs, it was impossible to find other sources that may have been of help.

Overall, the course received poor ratings from students. While it was recommended to all CIS majors, it was not recommended to those who need not take it.

This course provides an introduction to proof principles and logics, functions and relations, induction principles, combinatorics and graph theory, as well as a rigorous grounding in writing and reading mathematical proofs. It is the first course for the basic mathematical concepts and techniques needed in computer science.

Students found the course material to be very hard and frustrating. Describing the course as “dense” and “confusing,” many students felt the class lectures were “obscure” and lacking in examples, and the homework very difficult. Some students did not see the connection with the course and the rest of the CIS curriculum.

Students found Professor Gallier to be a “passionate,” “funny and engaging” teacher. Most students found that attendance to Professor Gallier’s office hours was required, because without him going over the homework in that time, the assignments were impossible.

Many students cited the difficulty of the homework, along with grading concerns for it. As many students got answers from office hours or copied solutions, the median homework scores were extremely high. This course is required for CIS undergraduates, and is recommended to be taken freshman year.

This course provides an introduction to proof principles and logics, functions and relations, induction principles, combinatorics and graph theory, as well as a rigorous grounding in writing and reading mathematical proofs.

Overall, the course was described as “brain-bending” and rewarding at the same time. Students considered it as “a good introduction to the mathematical basis of computer science.” Students had access to a very limited amount of material in preparing for the examinations, but found the class notes and review sessions to be particularly helpful. Some thought that not all topics covered were relevant and enlightening.

The majority of students liked Professor Mintz but some noted that he was seldom available outside of class. Students reported that the course TAs were extremely helpful in helping students get caught up in the material, but hoped that “they could have been more attentive to online posts.”

The course is a requirement for CIS majors and minors.

This course introduces proof principles and logics, functions and relations,
induction principles, combinatorics and graph theory, as well as a rigorous grounding in
writing and reading mathematical proofs.
Students stated that they learned “interesting and thought-provoking topics” in
this course. Students stated that this was “overall, this is a math course with computer
science applicability” that teach you to “think about things in different ways.” One
student stated, “this is a great course for anyone who wants to learn about proofs and
logic.” Students complained that this “course needs a textbook” and that “the course
needs a reference written from a perspective that is not the professor's.” Students also
complained about Professor Mintz's handouts of paper copies of homework since “Handouts and
paper assignments are annoying and can often get lost” and “refusing to post assignments
online.” Multiple students stated that they desired more online access for this course.
Students wished TA’s were more accessible.
Students universally praised Professor Mintz as “brilliant’ and “very eager to teach.”
One student suggested that “this class could use a recitation with smaller groups” because
although Professor “Mintz is an excellent professor, some people are afraid to ask what would
be considered ‘dumb’ questions in a large lecture.” Professor Mintz did not post office hours on his
website, but students reported that Professor Mintz as extremely willing and flexible to meet if you
emailed him directly. Students complained about the lack of TA office hours, and the
fact that the only way to communicate with them most of the time was through email.
This class is required for computer science majors and computer science minors.
It is usually taken freshman or sophomore year for computer science majors, but older
students often take this class to gain their computer science minor. Students universally
found this course to be “extremely fast-paced” and “challenging.” Students mentioned a
high workload with “23 difficult problem sets, 3 midterm exams, and 9 quizzes.”
Students highly recommended attending review sessions for high grades on the quizzes
and the exams. Students said this course was “very difficult” but “very rewarding if you
take the time.”

This course, titled “Mathematical Foundations of Computer Science, introduces basic mathematical concepts utilized in computer science. Topics discussed include proof principles and logic, functions and relations, induction principles, and combinatorics and graph theory.

Students expressed difficulty understanding the correlation between the material presented in this class and computer science, and desired that the class focus more on “concepts most relevant to computer science.”

Overall, students were unsatisfied with Professor Nenkova’s presentation of class material, with several lamenting that her lectures were “unhelpful,” “insipid” or “condescending.” Students were especially troubled when she “called out students” to answer questions and “publicly embarrassed them” for incorrect responses. However, a few students pointed out that the TAs were pleasant and helpful.

Students did not report unusual difficulties with completing homework, but many complained that the exam grading gave partial credit too infrequently. Students felt that this grading scheme made “memorizing the material” more important than understanding concepts.
