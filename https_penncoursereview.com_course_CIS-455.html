CIS 455 (Also: CIS-555 )

Internet & Web Systems

This course focuses on the challenges encountered in building Internet and web systems: scalability, interoperability (of data and code), security and fault tolerance, consistency models, and location of resources, services, and data. We will examine how XML standards enable information exchange; how web services support cross-platform interoperability (and what their limitations are); how to build high-performance application servers; how "cloud computing" services work; how to perform Akamai-like content distribution; and how to provide transaction support in distributed environments. We will study techniques for locating machines, resources, and data (including directory systems, information retrieval indexing, ranking, and web search); and we will investigate how different architectures support scalability (and the issues they face). We will also examine ideas that have been proposed for tomorrow's Web, and we will see some of the challenges, research directions, and potential pitfalls. An important goal of the course is not simply to discuss issues and solutions, but to provide hands-on experience with a substantial implementation project.

This course is about system design and development. A student commented on how this course could be split into two sections- “scalable web systems” and “internet systems.” Many different topics are covered in this course and some students desired more time to focus on certain topics, while encountering less breadth. Students remarked that homework descriptions were often very vague.

Prof. Haeberlen received high marks as being an expert in the subject matter. Students highly recommended seeing the professor or TA’s during office hours for homework assistance. Students wish there was a more defined rubric for homework grading and that that homework was weighted heavier than 25% of the total grade since it required an immense amount of time and effort.

This class is intended for upper level undergraduate students and graduate students. The graduate level version of this course is CIS 555. Strong Java programming skills along with familiarity with threads and concurrency were listed as prerequisite for this class. Students reported a very high workload and tough assignments for this class, which came with high academic payoff. Description of this course ranged from “the hardest class I ever took at Penn,” “2.0 credits worth of work, and “the most useful course I’ll ever take.” Students extremely recommended this course but only for computer science majors.

This course focuses on Internet and web technologies and the underlying principles of distributed systems, information retrieval, and data management. The material covered includes web and applications of server architectures, XML and semi-structured data, schema mediation, document indexing and retrieval peer-to-peer systems, distributed transactions and remote procedure calls. The course has a substantial group implementation project.  Overall, students found this course to be “extremely interesting” and “worthwhile.” They also felt that this course was very educational in that they learned many “useful concepts” throughout the semester. Students enjoyed the hands-on experience during the assigned projects.   Professor Haeberlen received mixed reviews. Some felt his lectures were intriguing, while others thought his slides could have been put together more clearly. Some students even felt that Professor Haeberlen should have given extra class sessions for the large number of students who were confused with the lecture material.   This class was extremely difficult and resulted in an “incredibly heavy workload.” Students noted that there was a lot of coding required for the assignments, and most of the work outside of class comprised of completing the major projects. However, the large amount of work was compensated for by “very fair exams.” Taking CIS 121 and 380 is recommended before enrolling in this class.
