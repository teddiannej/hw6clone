CIS 120

Prog Lang & Tech I

A fast-paced introduction to the fundamental concepts of programming and software design. This course assumes some previous programming experience, at the level of a high school computer science class or CIS110. (If you got at least 4 in the AP Computer Science A or AB exam, you will do great.) No specific programming language background is assumed: basic experience with any language (for instance Java, C, C++, VB, Python, Perl, or Scheme) is fine. If you have never programmed before, you should take CIS 110 first.

Fall 2010 PROG LANG & TECH I 001 77/82 2.38 2.55 2.94 2.95 3.41 2.25 2.50 2.38 2.29 3.60 1.59

CIS 120 introduced “the fundamental concepts of programming and software design” using java and python. The course has been described by students as “a good refresher course” that teaches both “theory and practice” and also introduces GUIs, more complicated data structures and Pythonic programming, but “doesn't teach many high-level aspects of programming.” The course has been restructured, and the new curriculum focuses on OCaml and Java, with more emphasis on design.

There were varied opinions about this course. A number of people felt that it was a good class. Several reported learning a lot of “directly useful” material enjoying the course overall. While some students thought that the course had an “excellent structure,” the majority felt that that it needed drastic restructuring. Some students felt that the material was “relevant and interesting” and that it “did a good job of explaining concepts.” However, others found it to be “tough” and “disappointing,” and they reported having to rely on their high school programming experience to get through it. On that note, those without previous programming experience struggled a great deal in this course. Students who had previously taken CIS 110 wished that “there had been a transition course.” However, those who took AP Computer Science AB felt that they should have been allowed to place out of this course.

There were also conflicting opinions about the lectures. While some students thought that they were “fairly interesting,” others felt that they were “dull” and “hard to digest.” Material covered in lectures “rarely corresponded to material on exams and in projects.” The labs were “no more useful” because there was no incentive to do anything in lab since the students “rarely coded” and lab assignments weren’t collected. One student reported that the homework assignments were “more useful than the lectures in terms of teaching the material.” Another stated that they “started off with ‘training wheels’ and become more open-ended as the course progressed.” Many students regarded the lectures as extremely “challenging” and “time consuming.” Most agreed that they were “disconnected” from the assignments and that the syntax needed to complete them was not taught before they were due. The biggest and almost unanimous complaint was that homework constituted only 20% of the grade but comprised 80% of the work for the course. Students reported that the exams required them to hand write code, which many found “unintuitive and counterproductive.” As a result, there was “a disconnect between what students can do (showed by their homework) and what they get on a timed test.”

Professor Marcus was described as “decent,” “entertaining,” and “a leader in his field.” While some students thought that he was approachable, they also felt that he was rarely helpful. Also, while some students reported that the professor explained the concepts and the material well, others felt that he went off on “random tangents” and “lost his train of thought” very frequently during lecture. Others noted that Professor Marcus didn’t appear to be familiar with his own slides.

Overall, the course received average ratings from students. While it is required for CIS majors, it was not recommended to those who need not take it. However, some students recommended it to math and physics majors, and others recommended it to “people who enjoy critical thinking.”

Spring 2010 PROG LANG & TECH I 001 103/107 2.55 2.75 3.01 2.93 3.51 2.27 2.60 2.62 2.43 3.68 2.01

This class introduces “the fundamental concepts of programming and software design” using java and python. Topics includes top-down program design, testing and debugging, interactive programming, iteration, recursion, data structures, and inheritance.

Students felt that the class was a good combination of “theory and practice,” with a few explicitly commenting that the use of java made the content feel more applicable. However, students were polarized towards the disconnect between lectures and the homework – some enjoyed spending time outside class learning more applied material for their homework, while others disliked bridging the gap outside of class.

Professor Marcus was described as “very entertaining” but also difficult to follow in class occasionally. Some students found lectures “hard to digest” because Professor Marcus sometimes had trouble remembering the correspondence between items on his lectures slides and the topics of discussion.

Homework was often described as a “disproportionately high percentage of work” that did not receive enough weight in the final grade. On the other hand, exams counted for a large portion of the final grade, yet students felt that being tested on writing code with paper and pencil did not capture well their understanding of the material. The course recommends that students have at least a high-school level background in computer programming, but does not require familiarity with java or python specifically.

The course is a fast-paced introduction to the fundamental concepts of programming. Previous programming at the level of high school computer science class or CIS 110 is required. While this course uses Java and Python, any experience with programming language, such as C/C++, VB, PHP, or Perl, is sufficient.

Students with more computer science background found the course to be very easy while those with less experience thought it was challenging but learned a lot. Overall, however they enjoyed the course and homework. Some students even stated it was their “favorite class this semester.” There were mixed reactions to the Python portion of the course; some felt it needed to be dropped while others thought it was very beneficial.

Students described Professor Pierce as “excellent,” “charismatic,” “fantastic,” and “very easy to understand.” However, one student noted while Professor Pierce was helpful, he was not always approachable due to the many changes in his office hours.

Students took this course for their major or minor requirements. A diagnostic quiz is given to students in the beginning to test if the course is suitable for their level. This course goes into greater depth about the topics covered in CIS 110.

A fast-paced introduction to the fundamental concepts of programming and software design. This course assumes some previous (non-language specific) programming experience, at the level of a high school computer science class or CIS110 (for instance Java, C, C++, VB, Python, Perl, or Scheme). Most students felt that the overall quality of this course was very high, although some had suggestions for the organization. While some felt that it was “well-structured,” others had problems with the emphasis on the homeworks. Many felt that this aspect of the course “was very well-organized.”

Because of the emphasis placed on the homework, not many students addressed the quality of Professor Weirich’s teaching. One student noted that Professor Weirich’s “main functionality was preparing a basic structure for the course and answering questions.” The same student also noticed that the TA’s taught a lot of the material and that “I’m 10x the programmer now than I was when I started.”

Many students noted that this course was difficult. One student described it as “ridiculously difficult for people without significant CS backgrounds.” Another student noted that the “work to value ratio [is] too high,” but another pointed out that it was “practical information” learned.

Programming Languages and Techniques I is a fast-paced introduction to the
fundamental concepts of programming and software design. This course assumes some
previous programming experience, at the level of a high school computer science class or
CIS110. No specific programming language background is assumed: basic experience
with any language (for instance Java, C, C++, VB, Python, Perl, or Scheme) is fine.
Students raved that this course was “rewarding” and “worth it.” The general
consensus between students was that this class was “truly challenging” but “challenging
in a good way” that made them “want to further pursue my interests in computer
science.” OCaml was taught at the beginning of the course to “level the playing field”
between people who had and had not taken 110, but a few students who were not as
experienced in computer science struggled a little more on assignments that those who
had prior experience breezed right by on.
Professor Zdancewic was seen as a “great teacher” and his course was well liked
by the majority of his students. However, students did wish that there were more TAs.
The course required a lot of work, but students viewed the material interesting and
were thus happy completing the work. Students should expect to spend upwards of 15
hours per week on this class, but also expect to learn a lot because of it and become better
at programming because of this course. Prior programming experience or CIS110 is
recommended.
