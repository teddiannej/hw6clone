CIS 240

Intro To Comp Systems

The goal of this course is to teach you how a computer really works. We begin by discussing transistors, the basic switching elements that constitute modern computers. We then describe how these transistors can be aggregated into more complex units like gates and ALUs and ultimately datapaths that perform computation. Once we have described how we can build a computer we will move on to talking about assembly language and how the computer is programmed at the lowest level. We will spend the second half of the course talking about the C programming language and how the features of this language are mapped onto the lower level assembly constructs.

This course teaches the bottom-level workings of a computer, beginning with transistors and simple computer hardware structures, and builds itself up to low-level programming, and eventually with computer architecture. It is a precursor to computer systems courses that expand on the content.

Students reported learning a lot from the class, although they felt it was a bit rushed and compressed. Students liked the overall practicality of the material taught except for a section that was created by the professor himself.

Students had mixed reviews about Professor Roth. Some considered him a poor teacher while others noted that once you got used to his sarcastic/humorous personality, he was very engaging and patient in explaining complex topics. All students agreed that they learned a lot from this course.

Students complained about the inordinate amount of work for this class as well as the difficult exam questions that were not similar to the homework. This course is a required class for most computer science related majors and has a prerequisite of CIS 110.

Fall 2011 INTRO TO COMP SYSTEMS 001 135/147 3.17 3.26 3.27 3.42 3.48 2.26 3.24 2.90 3.06 3.72 1.53

How do millions of transistors come together to form a complete computing
system? This bottom-up course begins with transistors and simple computer hardware
structures, continues with low-level programming using primitive machine instructions,
and finishes with an introduction to the C programming language. This course is a broad
introduction to all aspects of computer systems architecture and serves as the foundation
for subsequent computer systems courses, such as Digital Systems Organization and
Design (CIS 371), Computer Operating Systems (CIS 380), and Compilers and
Interpreters (CIS 341).
Students unanimously praised this class as “awesome”, “fascinating”, and
“infinitely educational.” They found the bottom up structure of the course from
transistors to C to be very effective.
Professor Taylor was well regarded by the students, who found him to be
“knowledgeable” and “funny.” They found his lectures to be clear and appreciated him
drilling important concepts throughout the semester.
Overall, students described this course as “tough” and “challenging.” The
homework assignments required a lot of time to complete, and required keeping up with
the lecture material. Many students felt that the difficulty increased greatly towards the
end of the semester, especially for the final homework. Even so, many students
commented that this was their favorite and “one of the most enlightening courses at
Penn.”

CIS 240 begins with transistors and simple computer hardware structures, continues with low-level programming using primitive machine instructions, and finishes with an introduction to all aspects of computer systems architecture and serves as the foundation for subsequent computer systems courses.

Students found this course to be “challenging” and “very difficult,” yet many felt that they “learned a lot in the course.” Although the material was “hard,” the course was described as “great,” “interesting,” “fascinating,” and “necessary.” Many students found the assignments to be “tricky and time-consuming” and complained about the lack of time given for harder assignments. Several students suggested revising the assignment due dates to give more time for harder projects. The assignments, however, were described as “effective at communicating key concepts.”

Students generally gave Professor Taylor rave reviews. Professor Taylor was described as “very accessible” and “an excellent teacher” who “communicates subject matter very clearly.” Students called the professor “passionate,” and they also noted that he was “friendly, funny, and overall seemed to enjoy teaching.” His “unparalleled enthusiasm” “made the material interesting and engaging.”

Although many students called this course “difficult and time consuming,” many also mentioned that it was one of the best classes they have taken at Penn. Students considered the material to be “extremely important” for any CIS major.
