CIS 505

Software Systems

This course provides an introduction to fundamental concepts of distributed systems, and the design principles for building large scale computational systems. Topics covered include communication, concurrency, programming paradigms, naming, managing shared state, caching, synchronization, reaching agreement, fault tolerance, security, middleware, and distributed applications. This course is appropriate as an upper-level undergraduate CIS elective.

This course provides an introduction to fundamental concepts of distributed systems, and the design principles for building large scale computational systems. Topics covered include communication, concurrency, programming paradigms, naming, managing shared state, caching, synchronization, reaching agreement, fault tolerance, security, middleware, and distributed applications. This course is appropriate as an upper-level undergraduate CIS elective.

Students thought that the "topics covered in this course were fascinating." Students found that they had to do a lot of self-learning when it came to doing the homework and projects, but nonetheless learned a lot from the class.

Students had generally positive comments about Prof. Blaze. Some thought Prof. Blaze was " [one of the] better teachers at Penn in the realm of teaching at School of Engineering" because he was an "engaging lecturer." Though students enjoyed having Prof. Blaze, they highlighted the fact that the class was "highly unorganized" and "poor structured." Students found it a recurring theme where Prof. Blaze would change "lecture topics without warning or clear distinction, and their contents were muddled with anecdotes and actual course material, followed with non-sequitur slides."

Students found that the "the class was not generally difficult" because the course did not cover a great amount of material. The homework for the class was not hard, but was time-consuming. Students found that his course was "very useful for CIS/CIT students." The listed prerequistes for this class are undergraduate-level knowledge of operating systems, networking, and programming experience (CIT 594 or equivalent).

Officially titled “Software Systems”, CIS505 provides an introduction to fundamental concepts of distributed systems. Topics covered include communication,  concurrency, programming paradigms, naming, managing shared state, caching, synchronization, reaching agreement, fault tolerance, security, middleware, and distributed applications.  Students generally found that the “course material [was] terribly dry.” They found that “the majority of the course was rather technical and difficult to relate to.” Their main concern, however, was the “very challenging/big final project,” which most considered to be somewhat unrelated to what they expected to be learning. They found that “[the] project requirements were also very vague and inconsistent at several points during the semester” and felt that they put more work into the course than the knowledge they got out of it.  Many students reported that “Professor Loo was a good teacher and very amiable.” However, some students felt that he put “far too little emphasis on formally reasoning about the correctness of the distributed systems [they] discussed in the course.” Students complained that he tried to fit too much information into a lecture at one time, reporting that “[when] slides have complex diagrams with small font on them and flash across the screen, or slides have 500+ words on them, something's wrong.”  This is a graduate level Computer and Information Science course. Students generally found there to be an excessive amount of work, especially with the final project. Students reported meeting with their group “five days a week, often for 5+ hours” for weeks. One frustrated student reported that his “research work ground to a halt; thank goodness [he] was not taking any other courses, or [he] surely would have failed them.” However, other students found that “[the] project [was not] extremely large, [but compressed] into a short time period.”
