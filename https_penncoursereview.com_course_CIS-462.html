CIS 462 (Also: CIS-562 )

Computer Animation

This course covers core subject matter common to the fields of robotics, character animation and embodied intelligent agents. The intent of the course is to provide the student with a solid technical foundation for developing, animating and controlling articulated systems used in interactive computer game virtual reality simulations and high-end animation applications. The course balances theory with practice by "looking under the hood" of current animation systems and authoring tools and exams the technologies and techniques used from both a computer science and engineering perspective. Topics covered include: geometric coordinate systems and transformations; quaternions; parametric curves and surfaces; forward and inverse kinematics; dynamic systems and control; computer simulation; keyframe, motion capture and procedural animation; behavior-based animation and control; facial animation; smart characters and intelligent agents.

This course covers core subject matter common to the fields of robotics, character
animation and embodied intelligent agents. The intent of the course is to provide the
student with a solid technical foundation for developing, animating and controlling
articulated systems used in interactive computer game virtual reality simulations and
high-end animation applications. The course balances theory with practice by "looking
under the hood" of current animation systems and authoring tools. It examines the
technologies and techniques used from both a computer science and engineering
perspective. Topics covered include: geometric coordinate systems and transformations;
quaternions; parametric curves and surfaces; forward and inverse kinematics; dynamic
systems and control; computer simulation; keyframe, motion capture and procedural
animation; behavior-based animation and control; facial animation; smart characters and
intelligent agents.
This course focused “on the math and physics foundations of computer
animation,” with math-intensive lectures and “difficult programming exercises.” Students
generally appreciated the content of the course, finding it to be “very challenging,” but
that they had “really learned a lot of useful knowledge.” A few respondents found that
going through example problems in class “to be the most helpful,” and would have liked
to have more of them covered.
One respondent praised Professor Lane for being “very nice and efficient,” while
another thanked him and his TAs for teaching many useful subjects in the course.
Professor Lane assigned a large amount of papers weekly for reading, and one
student felt that they were excessive. Although the homework was noted to be difficult,
generally students did not find the amount excessive. One student recommended it “to
everyone who will be involved with computer graphics in any capacity.”

This course covers core subject matter common to the fields of robotics, character animation and embodied intelligent agents. The intent of the course is to provide the student with a solid technical foundation for developing, animating and controlling articulated systems used in interactive computer game virtual reality simulations and high-end animation applications. The course balances theory with practice by "looking under the hood" of current animationsystems and authoring tools and exams the technologies and techniques used from both a computer science and engineering perspective. Topics covered include: geometric coordinate systems and transformations; quaternions; parametric curves and surfaces; forward and inverse kinematics; dynamic systems and control; computer simulation; keyframe, motion capture and procedural animation; behavior-based animation and control; facial animation; smart characters and intelligent agents.

Students generally found the course to be valuable for their major, and most felt that the content was useful and interesting. However, many students found the readings to be excessive and unnecessary.

Most students did not seem to enjoy Professor Lane’s teaching style, as many commented he covered content in a dry manner and manipulated equations in a way that became detached from the “big picture.”

Prerequisites for this course include linear algebra, curves and surfaces, dynamical systems, and 3D computer graphics. One student recommended significant background in C++.
