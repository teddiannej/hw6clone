CIS 502

Analysis Of Algorithms

An investigation of paradigms for design and analysis of algorithms. The course will include dynamic programming, flows and combinatorial optimization algorithms, linear programming, randomization and a brief introduction to intractability and approximation algorithms. The course will include other advanced topics, time permitting.

This course is an investigation of several major algorithms and their uses in areas
including list manipulation, sorting, searching, selection, and graph manipulation. The
course material also covers efficiency and complexity of algorithms, as well as
complexity classes.
Most of the students that took this course found it to be an overall great and “very
useful” course. Many students also found the textbook for the course to be excellent and
felt that it was a great complement to the lectures.
Students for the most part were satisfied with Professor Guha. However, they did
express that Professor Guha could have explained the difficult concepts more clearly and
in a way that would be easier to understand.
This is a “difficult” course that requires a lot of devotion to understanding the
material. There are also a lot of homework assignments that take a lot of time. Students
recommended this course to any graduate student with interest in algorithms, but
cautioned that students shouldn’t take too many difficult courses at the same time. The
prerequisite for this class is CIT 594 or an equivalent course.
Fall 2010 ANALYSIS OF ALGORITHMS 001 11/15 3.18 3.36 2.80 3.40 2.90 3.10 3.60 3.70 3.20 3.70 2.60

This class investigates several major algorithms and their uses in areas including list manipulation, sorting, searching, and graph manipulation. It also includes efficiency and complexity of algorithms and complexity classes.

Overall, students found the class to be enjoyable and useful. The material was described as very useful and enhanced the way of thinking about algorithms.

Students generally agreed that Professor Guha was “an excellent teacher” who stimulated interest in the topics presented. A few students proposed that Professor Guha should repeat student responses in class and present more problem-solving strategies. Some suggested including programming assignments in order to apply the concepts learned. One student complained that too much prior knowledge was required of incoming graduate students and that Professor Guha’s expectation for the students to do all the book problems was “unrealistic”.

This is a graduate level course. Undergraduates need permission to enroll. Students generally felt that the assignments were “hard, but fun.” The class, though tough, was regarded by many students as “excellent” and “very fundamental for this major”.
