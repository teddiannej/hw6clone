CIS 110

Intro To Comp Prog

Introduction to Computer Programming is the first course in our series introducing students to computer science. In this class you will learn the fundamentals of computer programming in Java, with emphasis on applications in science and engineering. You will also learn about the broader field of computer science and algorithmic thinking, the fundamental approach that computer scientists take to solving problems.

CIS 110 is a "Java lite" course that covers the fundamentals of object-oriented programming such as objects, classes, state, methods, loops, arrays, inheritance, and recursion using the Java programming language.

Students found that this class was a great introductory course for learning the fundamentals of computer science. Topics were not only covered in Java, but also in machine coding. Though students found the class difficult and highly work-intensive, it was a rewarding one because students felt that they had learn a lot of applicable skills and a new way of organized thinking.

Students enjoyed having Dr. Brown as a professor. They found him very engaging and very motivated to help his students learn. One student commented, "Dr. Brown genuinely cares that his students learn the material and is extremely accessible and helpful. He will stay late during office hours, answer questions on the discussion board, and go out of his way to teach." Though his lectures may be dry at times, students claim "he does his best" given the topic.

Students planning on taking this course should be prepared to spend upwards of twelve hours each week to complete the homework assignments. Students recommend that a good grasp of math and logic is necessary to complete the homeworks in a timely manner.

Spring 2011 INTRO TO COMP PROG 001 149/163 2.27 1.75 2.27 2.85 2.68 1.87 1.69 2.12 1.69 3.41 2.69

  CIS 110 explores such topics as how you program computers to accomplish tasks and how you break down complex tasks into simpler ones. CIS 110 is a "Java lite" course that covers the fundamentals of object-oriented programming such as objects, classes, state, methods, loops, arrays, inheritance, and recursion using the Java programming language.  The course begins with students learning Scratch as an introduction to programming. Each lecture is paired with a discussion group of about 15 students that meets once a week during the second weekly class time. Overall, students had negative reactions toward discussion groups, stating, that TAs are often too young to explain the material well and that discussion group leaders lacked enthusiasm. In addition, students were required to attend a two-hour lab once a week. Students generally felt that labs had the potential to be useful, but were often spent staring at the screen with no idea how to approach the problem.  Several students felt that Professor Griffin tried to convey the material in an exciting and interesting way, but that she fell short when it came to effectively and engagingly teaching the class. Some agreed that she appeared distant and out of touch, but noted that her interest in computer science was genuine and translatable.  Although an introductory Java course, many students who take it already have Java experience, resulting in a difficult class curve that favors those who should be in a higher-level course. A popular comment among students was that homework was a good reinforcement of skills and concepts but lectures had zero value for learning the concepts.
Fall 2010 INTRO TO COMP PROG 001 158/169 2.29 1.81 2.40 2.82 2.66 1.99 1.67 2.25 1.75 3.33 2.35

This course introduces core concepts of computer programming and computing to those with no experience. Concepts and techniques covered include algorithms, objects, state, methods, data types, expressions, testing, debugging, data abstraction, iteration, and basic data structures. Java is used as the modern object-oriented programming language to teach students how to solve real-world problems computationally with an emphasis on data modeling and algorithmic thinking. The course covers fundamentals of object-oriented programming such as objects, classes, state, methods, loops, arrays, inheritance, and recursion using the Java programming language. This course is a general requirement for Computer Science majors. It counts as a Tech Elective or Computer Science Elective for other Engineering students and as a Formal Reasoning course for College students. There are no midterms and finals for the course there are six tests spread out over the semester.   
Students seem to have enjoyed the class greatly. They described it to be demanding, but fair, very interesting and well constructed. Students generally agreed that the course contains very useful material and that they learned a lot, especially because it was very hands on. The course was a great introduction to programming concepts and very helpful and inclusive for a complete beginner. The basic concepts were taught in a fun way and there were lots of opportunities to practice. However, a few students felt that it was hard to follow if youve never programmed before and dont catch on right away. However, many students commented that even if it was tough at the beginning, it got easier once they got the hang of it. Some of them mentioned that they were inspired to delve deeper into other programming classes or decide to major or minor in computer science.  
The general consensus was that the labs and homework assignments were much more valuable and enjoyable than the lectures. The labs were highly informative, said one student, and allowed practice of concepts learned in class to give a very good practical introduction to computer science. The lab section, said most students, was where the true learning happened. Lecture was marginally useful for learning basics and helpful to see the process of writing code, but sometimes the students couldn't really see the connection between the activities in class and what [they] were learning. Many students felt that lectures were downright useless, although one student explained that this maybe due to the students' lack of willingness to come prepared to class.  
Nevertheless, the majority of the students called for more labs to replace lecture. Some even called for a fully lab-based course. Several students commented on the pair programming policy of the labs; while some found it extremely beneficial, others felt that working individually [would have benefitted them] more.  
Although some students felt that having six tests was less stressful than having a midterm and a final, others found the tests aggravating to the extreme. They felt that handwriting code in a short span of time was redundant and that test performance did not really correlate with programming ability. They also complained about the harsh grading system. [If you] miss one line of code, [you] get 30 points off. However, there was a lot of praise for the review sessions held by the TAs and the TAs in general, which were very helpful and knowledgeable.  
Most students loved Dr. Griffin as a teacher. They felt she was enthusiastic, understanding, and always willing to help; however, one student felt that she wasnt accessible as an instructor. Almost everyone agreed that she was very knowledgeable in computer science. There were various reactions to her teaching methods. Some students praised her demonstrations and attempts to make lectures interactive, while others commented on the cute examples she used with contempt. They felt like they were being treated like five year olds and felt that a more serious environment would be more conducive to learning the material. Some students also felt it was intimidating to participate [in her interactive games] in front of the whole class.   
Although most students had numerous complaints about the course, the general consensus appears to be that they more or less enjoyed it and learned a great deal from it. Students mentioned that a lot of time, thought and preparation went into the course [as obvious] from the homework assignments and lab sessions. Students learned an entirely new way of thinking, and were able to sharpen their problem solving skills. The course was highly recommended to everyone regardless of major.
