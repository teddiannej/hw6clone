CIS 121

Prog Lang And Tech II

This is an introductory course about Basic Algorithms and Data Structures using the Java programming language. We introduce elementary concepts about the complexity of an algorithm and methods for analyzing the running time of software. We describe data structures like stacks, queues, lists, trees, priority queues, maps, hash tables and graphs, and we discuss how to implement them efficiently and how to use them in problems-solving software. A larger project introducing students to some of the challenges of software development concludes the course.

This is an introductory course about Basic Algorithms and Data Structures using
the Java programming language. Elementary concepts about the complexity of an
algorithm and methods for analyzing the running time of software are introduced. Data
structures like stacks, queues, lists, trees, priority queues, maps, hash tables and graphs
are described, along with discussion on how to implement them efficiently and how to
use them in problems-solving software. A larger project introducing students to some of
the challenges of software development concludes the course.
Students were pleased with the content of the course, calling it “an essential
course for a CIS major” that “[prepared] individuals for technical interviews.” The class
was perceived to be “very difficult,” but instilled “a superb understanding [of] many
concepts that are required to move forward as a computer scientist.” Unfortunately, the
class was “extremely poorly organized.” Homework instructions were “vague” and
“constantly needed corrections,” making it difficult to get an early start on them.
A few students praised Professor Daniilidis for being “very approachable and
friendly.” However, students expressed the general sentiment that he was “underprepared
for class,” often “[making] a mistake on the board and [later retracting] his
statement, causing confusion in the class.” One student said that the TAs were “relatively
helpful,” but almost all others opined that they were “especially disorganized in the way
[they grade homework and interact with students],” and often provided contradictory and
“confusing answers on the discussion board,” where questions were “rarely [responded to
in] a timely manner.”
The class exams “were reasonable and fairly theoretical.” The majority of the
class felt that the homework and final project were “very difficult,” requiring a lot of selfreading,
but also serving as a good way to learn the course material. One student strongly
recommended non-CIS students against taking the class, saying that its “applications
outside of [computer science] are slim to none.”

CIS 121 is an introductory course about Basic Algorithms and Data Structures using the Java programming language. This class introduces elementary concepts about the complexity of an algorithm and methods for analyzing the running time of software. It describes data and discusses how to implement them efficiently and how to use them in problems-solving software. A larger project introducing students to some of the challenges of software development concludes the course.

Many students felt that the class material was “interesting” but that there was “no interesting way to teach it.” The exam was not “well-written” and the lectures were “extremely dull.” Several students reported being dependent upon outside material to complete homework assignments. One student felt that too much of the learning “happened on our own, in our own time.” However, many students appreciated that the class was not “too intense” and that the exams “weren’t too hard.”

According to a majority of students, the lectures were “low quality” and lacked “passion.” Students felt that Professor Daniilidis was a “very fair person” with “a nice sense of humor,” but that he was “unreachable outside of lecture, office hours, and recitation.” The quality of teaching was described as “a disappointment” and “subpar.”

Students generally agreed that this class was “very valuable” and “really good” for CIS majors. Many students did not understand why CIS 160 was a prerequisite, as material from the course was “hardly used.”

This review is based on the 7 student comments that were submitted.

This is an introductory course about Basic Algorithms and Data Structures using the Java programming language. We introduce elementary concepts about the complexity of an algorithm and methods for analyzing the running time of software. We describe data structures like stacks, queues, lists, trees, priority queues, maps, hash tables and graphs, and we discuss how to implement them efficiently and how to use them in problems-solving software. A larger project introducing students to some of the challenges of software development concludes the course.

Some students felt that the course was very disorganized and “poorly structured.” The subject matter, on the other hand, was well-liked by most students and “important concepts” are covered. “The information gained from this course is critical in getting internships in the technology and computer science industry,” one student noted. Most complaints had to do with the emphasis placed on the homeworks, as opposed to the actual content included in the syllabus.

Professor Stoyanovich was largely criticized just because of the weak organization of the course. Most TA’s were helpful and held productive office hours, but many students noted that “the grading seemed arbitrary for the homeworks.” Additionally, a few TA’s seemed “often clueless.”

Many students wished there were fewer homework and criticized the grading system for the homeworks. Some expressed the sentiment that the course is “challenging, “difficult,” or “time-consuming and frustrating.” Overall, though, students felt as though they learned a significant amount from this class.

This course builds on the concepts introduced in CIS 120. It focuses on data structures and introduces algorithm analysis such as software efficiency. It caps off with a large project in the end.

Students reported that most of the content is relatively boring and a repeat of AP Computer Science AB material. There was not enough implementation and too much usage of premade functions.

Students called “Val” a “friendly, accessible instructor.” Many enjoyed his lectures on the subpar material.

Students felt that the midterms were too easy and the curve too difficult. The mandatory attendance at 10 am was not well received and resulted in a lower instructor rating than the professor had hoped with such a requirement. The required text is Data Structures and Problem Solving Using Java by Mark Weiss.

This is an introductory course using Java. The course introduces students to elementary concepts about the complexity of an algorithm. Students are taught to implement data structures like stacks, queues, lists, trees, priority queues, maps, has tables, and graphs in problem-solving software. The final project is designed to introduce students to the challenges of software development.   Students generally found the material covered “interesting” and the course “valuable.” The course provided a “good foundation” for future CIS courses. The PowerPoint slides, which many students found “informative,” were posted online after each lecture. As a result, many students felt physical attendance was unnecessary.   In addition, students felt Dr. Taskar lacked the ability to engage students and “couldn’t communicate the information well.” Though Dr. Taskar was “quick to solicit questions” from his students, the lecture environment made it “intimidating to ask.” Many also felt that his office hours were scheduled at inconvenient times. In addition, students felt that the TAs were “condescending and unsupportive” and “rarely made themselves available.”   Students agreed that homework assignments, which were not assigned at regular intervals, were “very frustrating” and “unnecessarily difficult.” Assignments were often updated or corrected mere days before they were due, angering students. Multiple students reported that “even the TAs couldn’t complete” the assignments, which students also described as “misleading” and “horrendously designed.” In addition, students felt the grading was “unfair” and “absurd.”
