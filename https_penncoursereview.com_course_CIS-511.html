Topics covered in this course include finite automata, (deterministic and nondeterministic) regular graphs, regular expressions, regular grammars, Nerode congruence, the "pumping lemma", closure properties, and context-free languages. Some standard forms covered include: removal of e-rules, chain rules, and reduced grammars.Some normal forms covered include: context-free languages as fixed points (Ginsburg and Rose's Theorem), the Greibach Normal Form (using Rosenkrantz's matrix method), Ogden's Lemma, and pushdown automata (PDA's).

Students generally felt that the class material was difficult and theoretical, and that it required “a background in algebra”. Students were divided on the quality of course notes. Some thought that they were “slightly too condensed to really comprehend well”, while others felt they were “clear and concise”. Some students thought that the knowledge gained from this course would only be used in a very limited and advanced setting.

Most students enjoyed Professor Gallier’s teaching, espousing his enthusiasm, sense of humor, and “ability to explain very difficult and theoretical material in understandable ways”. Professor Gallier was extremely “enthusiastic about teaching beyond time” in office hours. Some even picked him out as the best professor they have ever had at Penn. However, some students felt that the lectures went too fast, so questions were discouraged as a result.

Students generally found the course challenging, with a heavy homework and project load. Most took the course as a requirement for their Computer Science Master’s, though some thought that the material was more suitable for those pursing a PhD and should thus be optional.

This course will cover: 1) review of regular and context-free languages and machine models; 2) Turing machines and RAM models, Decidability, Halting problem, Reductions, Recursively enumerable sets, Universal TMs, Church/Turing thesis; 3) time and space complexity, hierarchy theorems, the complexity classes P, NP, PSPACE, L, NL, and co-NL; 4) reductions revisited, Cook-Levin Theorem, completeness, NL = co-NL; as well as 5) advanced topics as time permits: Circuit complexity and parallel computation, randomized complexity, approximability, interaction and cryptography.

Most students enjoyed how the professor made the material accessible to everyone. Students noticed an overlap with this course’s material and CIS502. However one student stated how “I would have liked a more mathematical course, but the current format is definitely most appropriate for the intended audience.” Students also enjoyed the textbook which is “amazing, very detailed, and covered everything that was in the class.”

Students commended Prof. Kannan’s teaching style, which made the material accessible to students. Students also called the professor “exciting.” Students stated that most TA’s were incredibly helpful and highly recommended seeing them outside of class for homework assistance.

This course is intended for upper level undergraduate or graduate computer science students. Homework was reported as time-consuming and challenging especially at the beginning, but students were comforted by the fact that homework didn’t count much towards grading and really helped with understanding the class material.

The various topics covered in this course include: Finite automata (deterministic and nondeterministic) regular graphs, regular expressions, regular grammars, (Nerode congruence), the "pumping lemma", closure properties. Context-free languages. Standard forms: removal of e-rules, chain rules, reduced grammars. Chomsky Normal Form. Context-free languages as fixed points (Ginsburg and Rose's Theorem). Greibach Normal Form (using Rosenkrantz's matrix method). Ogden's Lemma and the "pumping lemma". Pushdown automata (PDA's). Equivalence of PDA's and context-free grammars. Brief sketch of top-down and bottom-up (nondeterministic) parsing. Deterministic PDA's. Closure properties. Partial recursive functions, Turing machines and RAM programs. Primitive recursion. Minimization. Equivalence of the models. Church/Turing's thesis. Acceptable Codings. A Universal RAM program. Undecidability of the halting problem. Recursively enumerable sets (RE sets).  Overall, students described the course material as “challenging but still accessible.” Most students learned a “huge deal” and found the problems to be “appealing and of broad interest.”  Professor Kannan was described as “a brilliant professor who managed to communicate traditionally dry, technical details in a clear, concise and often entertaining way.” The lectures were also “very clear” and Professor Kannan would often “provide thought-provoking examples in class which would help students to better understand the material.”   Generally, students found this course to be “very challenging.” The assigned homework was “very difficult.” This course was recommended for majors only.
