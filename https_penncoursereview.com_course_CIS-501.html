CIS 501

(Recent Example) Intro To Comp Arch

This course is an introductory graduate course on computer architecture with an emphasis on a quantitative approach to cost/performance design tradeoffs. The course covers the fundamentals of classical and modern uniprocessor design: performance and cost issues, instruction sets, pipelining, superscalar, out-of-order, and speculative execution mechanisms, caches, physical memory, virtual memory, and I/O. Other topics include: static scheduling, VLIW and EPIC, software speculation, long (SIMD) and short (multimedia) vector execution, multithreading, and an introduction to shared memory multiprocessors.

This course is an introductory graduate course on computer architecture with an
emphasis on a quantitative approach to cost/performance design tradeoffs. The course
covers the fundamentals of classical and modern uniprocessor design: performance and
cost issues, instruction sets, pipelining, superscalar, out-of-order, and speculative
execution mechanisms, caches, physical memory, virtual memory, and I/O. Other topics
include static scheduling, VLIW and EPIC, software speculation, long (SIMD), and short
(multimedia) vector execution, multithreading, and an introduction to shared memory
multiprocessors.
Students called the course “excellent” and “useful” but also “time-consuming”
and “difficult.” A student stated that “most of the material we covered seemed to be a
rehashing of material from CIS 371.” As a typical computer science course, there was a
lot of emphasis on the homework, and students had plenty of comments about them, “The
homework felt like a lot of data generation and plotting, and I spent more time on this
than the actual coding,” and “Even though the homework assignments are challenging,
they're always very helpful in getting you to understand in a very tangible way the
somewhat abstract concepts of architecture.”
Students had mixed reviews for Professor Martin. Some students found him to be
caring and easy to talk to. However, others felt that his attitude was rude and that his
lectures were slow-paced and relied too much on PowerPoint. Students also complained
about some dropped assignments due to time constraints.
This class is meant as an introductory graduate course for computer programmers.
Previous knowledge of computer programming is necessary. Students said the workload
for this class was heavy and time consuming. Students stated, “This is definitely the fall
core course to take,” and “highly recommended for anyone seeking a greater
understanding of computers.”

Students generally found the workload to be great. Knowledge of computer organization and basic programming skills are required for this course.
