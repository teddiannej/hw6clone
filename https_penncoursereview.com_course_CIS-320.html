CIS 320

Intro To Algorithms

How do you optimally encode a text file? How do you find shortest paths in a map? How do you design a communication network? How do you route data in a network? What are the limits of efficient computation? This course gives a comprehensive introduction to design and analysis of algorithms, and answers along the way to these and many other interesting computational questions. You will learn about problem-solving; advanced data structures such as universal hashing and red-black trees; advanced design and analysis techniques such as dynamic programming and amortized analysis; graph algorithms such as minimum spanning trees and network flows; NP-completeness theory; and approximation algorithms.

How do you optimally encode a text file? How do you find shortest paths in a map? How do you design a communication network? How do you route data in a network? What are the limits of efficient computation? This course gives a comprehensive introduction to design and analysis of algorithms, and answers along the way to these and many other interesting computational questions. The course will cover problem-solving; advanced data structures such as universal hashing and red-black trees; advanced design and analysis techniques such as dynamic programming and amortized analysis; graph algorithms such as minimum spanning trees and network flos; NP-completeness theory; and approximation algorithms.

This course is about how to design algorithms. Students enjoyed how this course made you really think, “The main benefit from this course is that it really makes you think! It is a very interesting course that shows how you can solve a problem much faster if you think about it in a smarter way!” Students stated that the textbook was a great way to refresh previously learned topics in computer science.

Prof. Khanna was lauded as a great professor. A student stated, “Professor Khanna did an excellent job of promoting student interest in a difficult subject matter to teach.” A few students desired for my examples were provided in class to supplement the class material.

This class is intended for juniors in the computer science department but can be taken by students who have completed the prerequisites CIS 120, 161, and 262. In general, students thought that homework was long but very informative. This course’s grading structure is based off 6 homework assignments, 2 midterms and a final. Several students stated that they wished homework were weighted higher in the grade since it is more representative of computer science programming skills. Students said this class was a “huge time commitment,” and “challenging” but still called the course “excellent,” “intellectually rewarding,” and “worth it.”

This course introduces the design and analysis of algorithms, focusing on problem solving, advanced data structures, advanced design and analysis techniques, graph algorithms, NP-completeness theory, and approximation algorithms.

Students overwhelmingly enjoyed the material in this class, calling it “informative” and “super interesting.” They appreciated the highly conceptual and logical basis for all of the knowledge from this class.

Similarly, students unanimously praised Professor Khanna, noting his “excellent” explanations of “difficult concepts or examples,” his wonderful “sense of humor,” and his generally “engaging lectures.” A few students suggested that proceeding in lectures a little more slowly or posting lectures notes could greatly enhance the amount of material understood; however, most found the pace satisfactory and ideal for maintaining interest.

Students judged this class to be extremely difficult, recommending it only to those who are “interested in the subject material and not afraid of a challenge.” Though the work was time-consuming, students concluded that it consistently “stimulated thought” and resulted in a worthwhile understanding of important topics.
